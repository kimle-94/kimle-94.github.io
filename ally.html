ALLY

WHAT IS ALLY?

Ally is an app that trains retail staff to recognize the signs of abuse and supports victims who seek a safe space. Ally helps victims escape abuse, harassment and violence of all kinds or receive personal support.

MY CHALLENGE

The retail staff ranges from very young people to senior people who are unfamiliar with mobile devices.

How might I design an app that would enable users of all ages to successfully use the training app without any frustration?

CONSTRAINT

After the first meeting with the client, I explored the iLa Generation website to gain a deeper understanding of the product.

My client provided me with very specific brand guidelines. In addition, the website was well designed with a bright and friendly theme that makes the users feel comfortable and confident.

As a result, my client and I decided to design the Ally app in accordance with the tone of the brand: friendly, interactive, engaging, and not super serious for all types of users.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

My client wanted the app to be a minimal design because the app is for everybody from youths to adults. Additionally, they suggested using Headspace and Calm as competitors since both are learning and meditation apps. As a result of conducting the competitive analysis research myself, here are three key insights for the Ally app:

Insight #1

Dark Mode

The Ally app features a training session with animated videos. As a result, the dark mode allows for users to concentrate on the training or meditation without distractions.

Insight #2

Training Process

There are four modules on the training page. Rather than swiping down, we can design swipe left cards to show more information and to conserve space.

Insight #3

A Process Circle

In the meditation and story sections, we could design an outside circle to represent a timeline, so that users could know how much time remains.

DESIGNING FROM SCRATCH

Here are the initial wireframes as design specifications and requirements from the iLa team, and my mission is to utilize my design expertise to make the app even better and more seamless.

Sketches

With all my research and insights in mind and initial wireframes from my client, I started building the app. After sketching out some ideas on paper , I transferred them to wireframes, sought feedback from my client, and then brought them to life in Adobe XD. We iterated and iterated until eaching the final decision.

Lo-fi wireframes

Not all sketches will result in a great design; therefore, I made some adjustments when creating the lo-fi wireframes to make the design better.

SETTING UP THE BOUNDARIES

Establishing the app's style guide

Once the lo-fi wireframes were complete, I moved on to the next step, which was to create the UI style guide for the app.

Creating components

The next step was to design the menu bars, buttons, and graphic elements of the app. My client wanted the entire app to be consistent and simple, so we spent a lot of time on graphic elements.

Graphic elements

After the 4th time feedback from my client, we chose the last design that had a minimal style while still highlighting the various sections with different colors.

Iconography

The next design element I created was a set of icons.

BRINGING IT ALL TOGETHER

High fidelity designs

The next step was to actually completed the app based on our insights, features, and goals. In this state, the app was divided into three parts: Sign-in & Sign up, Training Section, and Main Screens. The process was to design each part, send them to my client who conducted the user testing, and receive feedback from them. Following their feedback, I redesigned and sent the new versions to them. During this process, we iterated until we came to the final decision.

6 major improvements in my design

#1 Dark Mode

Based on insight #1 from the competitive analysis, I designed a dark mode for animated videos in Module 4.

>> When users click to start the animated videos, the background will change to dark blue to help them focus on the training without being distracted.

#2 Training Process

Based on insight #2 from the competitive analysis, I designed each card as a module. Each card shows the module name, the amount of time, a video introduction, and the status.

>> Users can keep track of their processes by checking the icons on the top left. They would understand that they need to complete one module before moving on to the next.

#3 Hearing a Story or Meditation

Based on insight #3 from the competitive analysis, I designed two listening screens: one for hearing a story, and the other for meditation.

>> As a result, the outer circle represents a timeline to indicate how much time has passed. Additionally, the two distinct designs will help users to distinguish whether they are listening to a story or meditating.

#4 Profile Screen

Both My Rewards and Training Process are equally important. In the two first versions, it was difficult for users to find information.

>> As a result, I designed two different-colored cards on a white background, so users could identify a given card's function instantly based on its colors

#5 Key Takeaway

There are four important key takeaways after completing all the scenarios. The first design failed to grab the attention of users.

>> Therefore, I designed key takeaways separately. By presenting them one by one, users will understand that these takeaways are noticeable and valuable.

#6 Animated Screens

Upon completing the training process, the users will receive their points as an encouraging reward.

>> Rather than designing dull and boring screens, I designed colorful and vibrant gifs for those screens. In this situation, small animations could cheer up the users much more effectively than a thousand words.

THE FINAL PRODUCT

BRINING THE APP TO LIFE

Interaction Design

After cleaning up the hi-fi prototype, I decided to animate the prototype using After Effects. My client would present their app and idea to investors, and they are afraid that the hi-fi prototype will not be able to convey the app completely and thoroughly to them. As a result, I created a video mockup to help them go beyond. Furthermore, the video mockup will show the user flow when using the app.

FINAL THOUGHTS

As a designer who strives to make user experiences as good as possible, here are some key takeaways I learned from this project:

Stakeholders could be valuable users. They are also trying to find a solution to a problem they had previously experienced. So understanding their perspective and thoughts could help designers better understand what the pain points are.

It is always worthy and valuable to listen to user feedback. It could not be a final solution until we repeatedly tested it with the users to come up with the final decision.

There is no a-ha moment without research and testing. Even when the iLa team reached out to me with the basic wireframes, I still did the research phase not to steal ideas or designs, but rather to gain a deeper understanding of the product in order to design it more effectively.

WHAT'D I DO NEXT?

Usability Testing

For this project, I can only do user testing with my client. If it were possible, I would like to test the product with actual users who are unfamiliar with it. By doing this, I would be able to narrow down the possible problems.

A Design Handoff

I handed over all of the necessary files with the product to the iLa team. At that time, my client was still looking for a developer team to implement the app. As a result, I did not have the opportunity to work directly with the developer team, so I do not know what I missed or what design will not work. I believe that designer success can be measured by how well they collaborate and communicate with developers as they handoff the design.
